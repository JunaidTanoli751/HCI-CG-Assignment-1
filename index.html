Welcome to HCI Exploration

What is Human-Computer Interaction?

Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) is the study of how people interact with computers and digital systems. It encompasses design, evaluation, and implementation of interactive computing systems for human use.

From the smartphone in your pocket to the ATM machine at your local bank, from the Daraz app you use for shopping to the Careem interface you rely on for rides‚Äîevery digital interaction is shaped by HCI principles.

Meet Don Norman

Donald Arthur Norman (born 1935) revolutionized how we think about design. His groundbreaking work transformed product design from engineer-centered to human-centered, emphasizing that good design makes technology invisible and intuitive.

"Good design is actually a lot harder to notice than poor design, in part because good designs fit our needs so well that the design is invisible."

‚Äî Don Norman, The Design of Everyday Things

Why Study HCI?

In today's digital Pakistan, understanding HCI is crucial because:

  1. Digital Economy Growth: With services like JazzCash, Easypaisa, and Bykea becoming essential, good UX design directly impacts millions of users daily.
  2. Accessibility Matters: Pakistan has diverse users‚Äîfrom tech-savvy youth to first-time smartphone users. Inclusive design ensures everyone can access digital services.
  3. Business Success: Companies like Foodpanda and Daraz succeed partly because of their intuitive interfaces. Poor UX means lost customers and revenue.
  4. Safety Critical: Medical apps, banking systems, and emergency services require flawless interaction design where errors can have serious consequences.

Core HCI Principles We'll Explore

üéØ Affordances
Visual cues that suggest how an object should be used. A button that looks "clickable" has good affordance.
üè∑Ô∏è Signifiers
Indicators that communicate where actions should take place. Icons, labels, and highlights are signifiers.
üó∫Ô∏è Mapping
The relationship between controls and their effects. Good mapping makes interfaces intuitive and predictable.
üí¨ Feedback
Information about the result of an action. Every user action should receive clear, immediate feedback.
üß† Mental Models
Users' understanding of how a system works. Design should match users' existing mental models from the real world.

Limitations that guide behavior and prevent errors. Physical, semantic, cultural, and logical constraints all play crucial roles.

Did You Know?

Fun Fact: The term "user-friendly" became popular in the 1980s, but Don Norman argued it's the wrong term. He said we should focus on making systems "activity-centered" rather than just "friendly."
